Introduction: Europe at the Start of the 20th Century

1900 – 1914

Background

By 1900, Europe was a continent of mounting tensions. Decades of industrialization had transformed national economies, while competing empires jostled for colonial territory around the globe. A complex web of alliances — the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain) and the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) — meant any local conflict risked becoming a continent-wide war.

  • Nationalism was reshaping empires and igniting ethnic conflicts, particularly in the Balkans.
  • Militarism had led to an arms race, especially between Britain and Germany in naval power.
  • Imperialism created rivalry over colonial territories in Africa and Asia.
  • Alliance systems locked nations into mutual defense obligations that could trigger a cascade of war declarations.

Section 02

World War I

1914 – 1918

Political Dimensions

The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary on June 28, 1914 by Gavrilo Princip triggered the alliance system. Austria-Hungary issued an ultimatum to Serbia; Germany gave Austria a "blank check" of support. Within weeks, most of Europe was at war.

  • Collapse of the German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, and Russian empires
  • Major leaders: Kaiser Wilhelm II, Tsar Nicholas II, David Lloyd George, Georges Clemenceau
  • The Fourteen Points and Wilson's vision for the peace

Military Dimensions

The Western Front became defined by trench warfare — miles of muddy trenches stretching from Belgium to Switzerland. New technologies transformed combat.

  • Trench warfare: Static front lines, "going over the top," shell shock
  • New weapons: Tanks (Battle of Somme, 1916), poison gas (Ypres, 1915), aircraft, machine guns
  • Major battles: Verdun, the Somme, Gallipoli, Jutland

Social Dimensions

WWI became the first "total war," mobilizing entire societies — not just armies.

  • Women entered the workforce in large numbers, laying groundwork for suffrage movements
  • Government propaganda shaped public opinion and recruitment
  • Rationing and food shortages affected civilian populations
  • The psychological toll: shell shock and the "Lost Generation"

Economic Dimensions

The war placed enormous financial strain on all belligerents.

  • Wartime production demanded conversion of civilian industries
  • Britain and France borrowed heavily from the United States
  • Germany's economy was strangled by the Allied naval blockade
  • War debts would destabilize European economies throughout the 1920s

Section 03

The Interwar Period

1919 – 1939

Political Instability

The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed harsh terms on Germany: war guilt clause, massive reparations, territorial losses, and military restrictions. Rather than creating lasting peace, the treaty bred resentment that extremist politicians would exploit throughout the 1920s and 30s.

Economic Instability: German Inflation & Unemployment

Rise of Dictatorships

Economic devastation from WWI debt and the Great Depression created fertile ground for authoritarian ideologies.

  • Fascism (Italy, 1922): Mussolini's March on Rome; glorification of the state and violence
  • Nazism (Germany, 1933): Hitler's rise via democratic elections; antisemitism, ultranationalism, Aryan supremacy
  • Authoritarianism across Europe: Franco in Spain, Horthy in Hungary, Pilsudski in Poland

Section 04

World War II

1939 – 1945

Political Dimensions

Hitler's expansionist foreign policy — rearmament, remilitarization of the Rhineland, Anschluss with Austria, annexation of Czechoslovakia — went unchallenged through a policy of appeasement. The invasion of Poland on September 1, 1939 finally triggered British and French declarations of war.

  • Allied leaders: Churchill, Roosevelt, Stalin ("The Big Three")
  • Axis powers: Germany, Italy, Japan
  • The Holocaust: systematic genocide of 6 million Jews and millions of others

Military Dimensions

  • Blitzkrieg: Fast, coordinated attacks using tanks, aircraft, and infantry
  • Battle of Britain (1940): First major battle fought entirely by air forces
  • Operation Barbarossa (1941): German invasion of the USSR — turning point
  • D-Day (June 6, 1944): Allied invasion of Normandy
  • Fall of Berlin (May 1945): End of war in Europe

Social Dimensions

  • Civilian bombing campaigns (the Blitz, Dresden) blurred the line between home front and battlefield
  • The Holocaust — Nazi genocide of European Jews, Roma, disabled, and others
  • Resistance movements across occupied Europe
  • Propaganda on all sides to maintain morale and dehumanize the enemy

Economic Dimensions

  • Total economic mobilization — "Arsenal of Democracy" (U.S. Lend-Lease)
  • Nazi war economy: slave labor, plunder of occupied territories
  • Marshall Plan (post-war): U.S. funding to rebuild Western Europe
  • Bretton Woods (1944): established new international monetary order

Section 05

The Cold War & European Division

1945 – 1991

Post-War Europe

After WWII, Europe was divided between Western liberal democracies allied with the United States and Eastern communist states under Soviet influence. Churchill described this divide as an "Iron Curtain" descending across the continent.

  • NATO (1949): North Atlantic Treaty Organization — Western military alliance
  • Warsaw Pact (1955): Soviet-led military alliance of Eastern bloc nations
  • Berlin Wall (1961–1989): Physical symbol of the Iron Curtain; divided East and West Berlin
  • Proxy conflicts: Korea, Vietnam, Angola — superpowers fighting through allied states
  • Nuclear tension: Cuban Missile Crisis (1962), arms race, MAD doctrine
